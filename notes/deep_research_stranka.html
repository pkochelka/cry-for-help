Scientific Domain Knowledge

Understanding tear ferning and desiccates to inform feature engineering.

What is Tear Desiccation?

When human tear fluid is allowed to dry (desiccate) under controlled conditions, the interaction between salts, proteins (like mucin), and lipids causes crystallization. This phenomenon is often called "Tear Ferning" because healthy tears form dense, fern-like branch structures.

In patients with chronic diseases (e.g., Dry Eye Syndrome, Diabetes Mellitus, Cystic Fibrosis), the biochemical composition of the tear alters. This disrupts the crystallization process, leading to sparse ferns, fragmented crystals, or amorphous blobs.

Rolando's Grading Scale (Standard Baseline)

  • Type I & II: Healthy. Dense, uniform, highly branched ferns.
  • Type III: Transitional. Sparse branching, larger spaces between crystals.
  • Type IV: Pathological. No ferns, complete structural breakdown, isolated crystal clumps.
🔬

Why SPM (Scanning Probe Microscopy)?

Standard light microscopy only shows 2D shapes. SPM provides 3D nanoscale topography. This means you have access to height, roughness, and volume of the crystals, not just their outline.

  • → Z-Axis Data: Crystal thickness indicates protein concentration.
  • → Surface Roughness (Sq, Sa): High variance in diseased states.
  • → Fractal Dimension: Quantifies the complexity of the branching.

Phase 1: SPM Preprocessing

Raw SPM/AFM data has tilt, scanner bow, and noise artifacts. Feeding this raw into an ML model will cause it to learn the artifacts rather than the biological features.

  • Mean plane subtraction (Leveling).
  • Polynomial background subtraction (Flattening).
  • Thresholding to isolate the crystals from the substrate base.

Combating Imbalance (The F1 Trap)

Medical datasets are rarely 50/50. If 80% of samples are healthy, a model predicting "always healthy" gets 80% accuracy, but a terrible F1-Score on the minority class. Because the metric is Weighted F1, you must perform well on BOTH classes, weighted by their support.

Crucial Steps:

  • 1. Use Stratified K-Fold CV (e.g., 5-fold) to ensure class distributions are identical across splits.
  • 2. Use Class Weights in your loss function (e.g., `scale_pos_weight` in XGBoost).
  • 3. Threshold Optimization: Don't just use 0.5 for classification. Use the validation set to find the probability threshold that maximizes the Weighted F1 score.
